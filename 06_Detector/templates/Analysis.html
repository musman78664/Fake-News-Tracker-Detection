Question

Can machine learning be used to detect fake news? How does the dataset affect the efficacy of the model/app?

Findings

The models used in this app determine whether a news article (headline or body) by words and structure. If cannot fact check. The ML models might categorize a genuine-sounding article as factual, even if it’s full fictional information. And, as is, certain words can trick the models. “GOP,” for example, is almost exclusively found in headlines labeled “False” in our datasets. Words and phrases like “Supreme Court” are found significantly more often in true articles. The presence of these heavier weighted words and phrases may skew the resulting prediction.

The longer the article, the more accurate the model will be. For headlines, our models achieved about 85% accuracy. For body copy, which are much longer, our models achieved accuracy and F1 scores in the mid-90s. Models trained with body copy as the sole feature is, therefore, significantly more difficult to fool.

Method

We used Scikit-Learn‘s TfidfVectorizer to extract tf–idf features for the headline and body features. After splitting the data into training and testing segments, we fit it to SVM, PAS, KNN, NB models. Regardless of dataset or feature, sklearn’s Support vector machine and PassiveAggressiveClassifier() models performed the best using the resulting tf–idf data. Using grid search to hypertune our SVM model, we determined the best parameters to use. We found linear to be the most effective kernel, followed closely by RBF.

We created multiple pipelines using the vectorizer and best-performing models (linear SVM and PassiveAggressiveClassifier), and saved the result to be deployed using the Flask-powered app.

Conclusion

ML can be used to detect fake news, but it can be easily fooled.

For better real-world performance, we should look into sentiment analysis and other NLP techniques. These might help detect bias and other factors that the current models are missing.
